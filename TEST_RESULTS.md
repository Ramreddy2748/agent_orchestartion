## Question

# 1. What are the requirements for quality improvement programs?

## Answer

### Quality Improvement Programs Requirements

## Overview
Quality Improvement Programs are a critical component of a hospital's Quality Management System (QMS) and Quality Assessment and Performance Improvement (QAPI) program. These requirements ensure hospitals systematically examine the quality of care delivered and implement specific improvement projects on an ongoing basis.

---

## Core Requirements

### 1. Quality Management System Development & Implementation
- **Requirement**: Organizations must develop, implement, and maintain an ongoing, hospital-wide, data-driven effective Quality Management System (QMS)
- **Scope**: The QMS must integrate the Quality Assessment and Performance Improvement (QAPI) program
- **Purpose**:
  - Manage and improve performance
  - Enhance patient safety
  - Improve overall quality of patient care and operations

### 2. Leadership Responsibility & Accountability
The governing body, medical staff, and administrative officials are responsible and accountable for:

#### Key Accountability Functions:
- Periodic review of the QMS
- Development of a plan to implement and maintain the QMS
- Review of the progress of QAPI projects
- Determination of annual QAPI projects
- Evaluation of the effectiveness of improvement actions
- Ensuring clear expectations for safety are established and communicated hospital-wide
- Allocating adequate resources for QMS and QAPI program requirements

#### Leadership Structure:
- Hospital governing body (or organized group with full legal authority)
- Medical staff (or medical staff executive committee if delegated)
- Senior leadership including:
  - Chief Executive Officer (CEO)
  - Chief Operating Officer (COO)
  - Chief Nurse Executive (or equivalent)
  - Other hospital administrators

### 3. Safety Expectations & Communication
- Clear expectations for safety must be established and communicated hospital-wide
- All staff (including contract staff and volunteers) must adhere to organizational policies and procedures
- All clinical staff must implement or clarify patient care orders as needed
- Safety expectations must be communicated to those providing services under arrangements or contracts
- Safety communication should be documented in contracts

### 4. Resource Allocation
- Adequate resources must be allocated for:
  - Measuring organizational performance
  - Assessing performance
  - Improving performance
  - Sustaining improvements
  - Reducing risk to patients

#### Resource Considerations:
- Resources must be commensurate with the scope and complexity of services provided
- Resources include:
  - Sufficient staff (including consultants as needed)
  - Staff time
  - Information systems
  - Education and training
- Staff must be qualified (experienced or trained) for assigned functions
- Hospital should demonstrate program inclusion in budget process
- Evidence of allocated staff dedicated to program management and oversight

### 5. Corrective & Preventive Actions
- Corrective and preventive actions must be implemented
- Actions must be measured and monitored
- Effectiveness and sustainability must be verified

### 6. Performance Improvement Projects
These are differentiated from continuous quality improvement activities:

#### Selection Criteria:
- Projects must focus on various processes, functions, and areas of the organization
- Number and scope of projects must be proportional to:
  - Scope of organization's operations
  - Complexity of organization's operations
  - Services offered

#### Annual Project Requirements:
- Projects must be conducted annually
- Projects must be documented to include:
  - Rationale for selection
  - Measurable progress achieved
- Projects require significant up-front planning
- Projects must include defined objectives
- Projects must have a definitive beginning and end date (time-limited)

#### Project Documentation:
- Hospital must keep records on each performance improvement project from the previous survey cycle
- Must maintain a list of currently underway projects
- Documentation must include:
  - Explanation of why the project was undertaken
  - Data supporting project selection (internal data, publicly available data, or recommendations from nationally recognized organizations)
  - For ongoing projects: explanation of activities and impact monitoring
  - Baseline data collection for outcome assessment
  - For completed projects: evidence of measurable progress toward improving quality of care or patient safety

### 7. Quality Improvement Organization (QIO) Participation (Optional)
- Hospitals may participate in QIO cooperative projects to fulfill annual project requirements
- If participating in QIO:
  - Must demonstrate that information and supporting documentation is provided to the QIO
- If not participating in QIO:
  - Projects must be of comparable effort to QIO projects
  - Must consider:
    - Number of patients affected
    - Range of services covered
    - Projected magnitude of benefit to individual patients

---

## Special Requirements

### Obstetrical Services
For hospitals that offer obstetrical services, additional requirements apply:

#### Data Analysis Requirement:
- Hospitals must utilize their QAPI program to analyze data and quality indicators collected by diverse subpopulations among obstetrical patients
- Implementation deadline: January 1, 2027

#### Measurable Performance Improvement:
- Must conduct at least one measurable performance improvement project annually focused on:
  - Improving health outcomes
  - Reducing disparities
  - Among the hospital's population(s) of obstetrical patients
- Implementation deadline: January 1, 2027

### Information Technology Systems
- Hospitals may develop and implement IT systems explicitly designed to improve patient safety and quality of care
- In the initial development stage, these projects do not need to demonstrate measurable improvement in health outcome indicators

---

## Compliance Requirements

### Ongoing Compliance
- Organizations must comply with all Quality Management System standards at all times as part of their QMS

### ISO 9001 Methodology
- Until the organization achieves ISO 9001 Compliance/Certification, the organization must follow at a minimum the ISO 9001 methodology as specified in standards

### Staff Adherence
- All staff must adhere to organizational policies and procedures
- All clinical staff must implement or clarify patient care orders

---

## Continuous Improvement Activities vs. Projects

### Performance Improvement Activities (Continuous)
These are ongoing functions of a hospital QMS related to quality and patient safety:
- Ongoing tracking of medical errors and adverse events
- Analysis of data
- Implementation of changes with associated education and training
- Continuous monitoring of quality and safety in all hospital departments and service areas

### Performance Improvement Projects (Time-Limited)
These require:
- Significant up-front planning
- Defined project objectives
- Definitive beginning and end dates
- Documented rationale for selection
- Evidence of measurable progress

---

## Summary of Key Principles

1. **Hospital-Wide Commitment**: QMS and QAPI programs must be integrated and hospital-wide
2. **Data-Driven Approach**: All improvement efforts must be based on data analysis
3. **Leadership Oversight**: Senior leadership must actively engage in planning, monitoring, and evaluating improvement efforts
4. **Adequate Resources**: Organizations must allocate sufficient resources commensurate with their complexity
5. **Annual Projects**: Organizations must conduct and document improvement projects annually
6. **Measurement & Sustainability**: All improvements must be measured and monitored for sustainability
7. **Communication**: Clear safety expectations must be communicated to all staff and contractors
8. **Documentation**: All projects and activities must be documented with clear rationale and measurable outcomes
9. **Equity Considerations**: Hospitals must analyze data by diverse subpopulations (particularly for obstetrical services)
10. **Compliance**: Organizations must maintain ongoing compliance with all QMS requirements

---

## Source
Healthcare Standards - Quality Management System (QM.1)

## Question

# 2. Describe the infection control requirements for surgical areas

## Answer

### Infection Control Requirements for Surgical Areas

## Overview
Infection control in surgical areas is critical to patient safety and requires comprehensive policies, procedures, and adherence to nationally recognized standards. These requirements encompass aseptic practices, environmental controls, equipment sterilization, and personnel training.

---

## Foundational Requirements

### 1. Adherence to National Standards
Surgical services must be provided in accordance with acceptable nationally recognized standards of practice and guidelines, including but not limited to:
- **AORN** - Association of periOperative Registered Nurses
- **CDC** - Centers for Disease Control and Prevention
- **APIC** - Association for Professionals in Infection Control and Epidemiology
- **ASA** - American Society of Anesthesiologists
- **AANA** - American Association of Nurse Anesthetists
- **AAMI** - Association for the Advancement of Medical Instrumentation
- Other applicable professional organizations based on scope and complexity of services

### 2. Well-Organized Surgical Services
- Services must be well organized and appropriate to the scope offered
- Quality of outpatient surgical services must be consistent with inpatient care per complexity of services
- Services designed to assure achievement and maintenance of high standards of medical practice and patient care
- Services must be consistent with organizational needs and resources

---

## Core Aseptic and Infection Control Practices

### 1. Aseptic Surveillance and Practice
Policies and procedures must include:
- **Aseptic surveillance and practice requirements**
- **Scrub techniques** for surgical personnel
- Identification of infected and non-infected cases
- Clear protocols for maintaining sterile field throughout surgical procedures

### 2. Environmental Controls
The organization must develop and implement policies for:
- **Monitoring of temperature and humidity** in surgical areas
- Housekeeping requirements and procedures
- Maintenance of clean surgical environment
- Control of air quality and particle filtration
- Prevention of cross-contamination

### 3. Traffic Flow and Access Control
- Access to operative and recovery areas must be **limited to authorized personnel**
- Traffic flow patterns must adhere to accepted nationally recognized standards
- Movement into and out of surgical areas must be controlled to minimize contamination risk

---

## Surgical Attire and Personnel

### 1. Operating Room Attire Requirements
Personnel working in surgical areas must comply with:
- Only clean surgical costumes shall be worn in the operating room
- Surgical costumes designed for **maximum skin and hair coverage**
- Attire must be suitable for the kind of surgical case performed
- Organization policies specify acceptable operating room attire
- Proper use of caps, gowns, masks, and other attire as required

### 2. Personnel Training and Competency
- All surgical personnel must receive competency-based training on aseptic practices
- Training must cover practical applications of infection prevention and control guidelines
- Education required for:
  - Scrub techniques
  - Sterile field maintenance
  - Infection prevention practices
  - Proper use of personal protective equipment

---

## Sterilization and Disinfection

### 1. High-Level Disinfection Procedures
Policies and procedures must include:
- Development and implementation of sterilization and high-level disinfection procedures
- Procedures must follow nationally recognized standards of practice
- All sterilization equipment must be properly monitored, inspected, tested, and maintained
- Documentation of all sterilization activities

### 2. Surgical Instrument Reprocessing

#### General Requirements:
- All surgical instrumentation and medical devices must be reprocessed according to:
  - **Manufacturer's Instructions for Use (IFU)** - must be readily available to all staff
  - Professionally recognized standards of practice
  - Organizational policies

#### IFU Access:
- Manufacturer IFUs for all of the following must be readily accessible:
  - Device manufacturers
  - Chemicals utilized for disinfection and sterilization
  - Sterilizer manufacturers
  - Containment device manufacturers (rigid and soft containment)

#### Conflict Resolution:
- Organization must have a process in place for resolution of conflicting manufacturer IFUs

#### Documentation:
- Maintenance and retention of reprocessing activity documentation according to:
  - Professionally recognized standards of practice
  - Organizational policies

### 3. Processing Timeline and Workflow
- Adequate time must be allowed for reprocessing
- All steps recommended by device manufacturer must be followed, including:
  - Pre-cleaning
  - Cleaning
  - Drying
  - Inspection
  - Packaging
  - Proper storage

### 4. Sterilized Material Storage and Handling
- Sterile materials must be packaged, labeled, and stored to ensure sterility
- Storage environment must be:
  - Moisture-controlled
  - Dust-controlled
- Policies and procedures for expiration dates must be developed and followed
- Handling processes must maintain sterility throughout storage and transport

### 5. Immediate-Use Steam Sterilization (IUSS)

#### Permitted Use:
- IUSS may be used ONLY for:
  - Urgent situations
  - Emergent situations
  - NOT for routine schedule management or insufficient inventory

#### Implantable Devices:
- IUSS of implantable devices shall be limited to **emergent situations only**

#### Traceability Requirements:
- All occurrences of IUSS must be **traceable to the patient**
- Must include documentation of:
  - Justification for urgent/emergent use
  - Nature of the procedure
  - Date, time, and personnel involved
  - Surgeon's notes and OR documentation

#### Process Monitoring:
- Organizations must have defined processes to limit and/or eliminate IUSS use
- Excessive late requests may indicate process failures and patient safety issues
- Patterns should be identified and corrective actions implemented

### 6. Gastrointestinal Endoscope Reprocessing

#### High-Quality Process Requirements:
- Organization policy must ensure a reliable, high-quality process for endoscope reprocessing
- Process must minimize infection risks

#### Traceability:
- Process in place to identify which gastrointestinal endoscope was used on each patient
- All endoscope-patient connections must be documented

### 7. Non-Hospital-Owned Equipment

#### Acceptance and Inventory Process:
- Organization must have documented process for all items not solely owned by the organization:
  - Acceptance of items
  - Inventory of items
  - Documentation of Manufacturer's IFU on all items
  - Documented training for use and reprocessing of items

---

## Handling of Surgical Specimens and Waste

### 1. Surgical Specimen Handling
Policies and procedures must include:
- Proper handling, care, and labeling of surgical specimens
- Documentation and chain of custody procedures
- Appropriate storage and transport

### 2. Infectious and Biomedical/Medical Waste
Policies must address:
- Proper identification and segregation of infectious waste
- Safe handling procedures
- Appropriate containment
- Disposal in accordance with regulatory requirements
- Personnel protection during handling

---

## Infection Prevention and Control Program Integration

### 1. Surveillance and Monitoring
- Systems must be in place and operational for tracking of:
  - All infection surveillance activities
  - Prevention and control activities
  - Implementation, success, and sustainability of such activities

### 2. Coordination with QAPI
- Healthcare Associated Infections (HAIs) identified in surgical areas must be addressed in collaboration with organization QAPI leadership
- Infection prevention and control issues must be communicated and collaborated on with QAPI program

### 3. Training and Education
Competency-based training and education must be provided to:
- All surgical staff (medical and nursing)
- Contract personnel
- All personnel providing services in surgical areas
- Training must include practical applications of infection prevention and control guidelines, policies, and procedures

### 4. Auditing and Adherence Monitoring
- Active auditing of adherence to infection prevention and control policies and procedures
- Regular monitoring of surgical personnel compliance with aseptic practices
- Documentation of audit findings and corrective actions

### 5. Communication and Collaboration
- Regular communication between infection prevention and control professionals and surgical leadership
- Collaboration with antibiotic stewardship program
- Reporting of infection-related issues to organizational leadership
- Implementation of corrective action plans

---

## Supporting Infrastructure

### 1. Equipment and Facilities
- Organization must have equipment available for:
  - Rapid sterilization of operating room materials
  - Routine sterilization of operating room materials
- All sterilization equipment must be:
  - Monitored
  - Inspected
  - Tested
  - Maintained by the organization's biomedical equipment/clinical engineering program

### 2. Surgical Procedure Protocols
- Procedure-specific or general protocols appropriate for all surgical procedures performed
- List of equipment, materials, and supplies necessary to properly carry out surgical services
- Protocols must address infection prevention at all stages of procedure

### 3. Safety Practices
Policies and procedures must include:
- Fire safety in surgical areas
- Site marking and verification procedures
- Time-outs and verification processes
- Malignant Hyperthermia (MH) rescue capability in all:
  - Surgical/procedural areas
  - Any area using volatile anesthetic agents
  - Areas where succinylcholine is used

---

## Specialized Requirements

### 1. Identification of Infected Cases
- Policies must include identification processes for:
  - Infected cases
  - Non-infected cases
- Appropriate precautions and protocols based on infection status

### 2. Foreign Body Prevention
- Organization must have a process in place to ensure that **no foreign bodies are retained** in patients following surgical procedures
- Surgical count procedures in accordance with accepted nationally recognized standards of practice and guidelines

### 3. Environmental Monitoring
- Regular monitoring of surgical area conditions:
  - Temperature and humidity maintenance
  - Air quality and filtration
  - Cleanliness and disinfection status
- Documentation of monitoring activities

---

## Leadership Responsibilities

### 1. Governing Body Accountability
- Ensure systems are in place for tracking infection surveillance, prevention, and control activities
- Ensure adequate resource allocation
- Ensure coordination with QAPI program
- Hold leadership accountable for implementation and sustainability of infection control measures

### 2. Infection Preventionist Responsibilities
- Development and implementation of organization-wide infection surveillance, prevention, and control policies
- Communication with surgical leadership and staff
- Competency-based training and education
- Active auditing of surgical personnel compliance
- Collaboration with antibiotic stewardship program

### 3. Surgical Leadership Responsibilities
- Implementation of infection control policies and procedures
- Staff training and competency verification
- Equipment maintenance and monitoring
- Reporting of infection-related issues
- Cooperation with infection prevention personnel

---

## Key Principles Summary

1. **Nationally Recognized Standards**: All infection control practices must align with recognized standards (AORN, CDC, APIC, etc.)
2. **Aseptic Technique**: Strict adherence to aseptic practices and scrub techniques is mandatory
3. **Environmental Control**: Temperature, humidity, and air quality must be monitored and maintained
4. **Personnel Training**: All surgical personnel must receive competency-based infection control training
5. **Equipment Management**: Sterilization equipment must be properly monitored, tested, and maintained
6. **Manufacturer Instructions**: All reprocessing must follow manufacturer's IFU precisely
7. **Documentation**: All sterilization, infection surveillance, and control activities must be documented
8. **Limited IUSS Use**: Immediate-use steam sterilization limited to urgent/emergent situations only
9. **Traceability**: All IUSS procedures and endoscope uses must be traceable to specific patients
10. **Integration**: Infection control must be integrated with QAPI program and organizational quality initiatives
11. **Continuous Monitoring**: Ongoing auditing, monitoring, and assessment of surgical area infection prevention practices
12. **Communication**: Clear communication between infection prevention, surgical staff, and organizational leadership

---

## Source
Healthcare Standards - Surgical Services (SS.1, SS.10) and Infection Prevention and Control Program (IC.3)


## Question

# 3. How should hospitals handle medication errors?

## Answer

### How Hospitals Should Handle Medication Errors

## Overview
Medication errors are a critical patient safety concern and must be managed through comprehensive policies, immediate reporting, investigation, analysis, and corrective action. Hospitals are required to develop systems that detect, report, investigate, and learn from medication errors to prevent future occurrences and protect patient safety.

---

## Foundational Responsibilities

### 1. Medical Staff Leadership
- **The medical staff is responsible for developing policies and procedures that minimize drug errors**
- This responsibility may be delegated to an organized pharmacy oversight group
- Medical staff must ensure involvement and approval of all medication error prevention policies and procedures

### 2. Pharmacy Leadership
- Directed by a full-time, part-time, or consulting registered pharmacist
- Responsible for developing, supervising, and coordinating all pharmacy service activities
- Must ensure adequate number of qualified personnel for effective medication management services

### 3. Organizational Leadership
- Governing body must ensure systems are in place for medication error tracking, analysis, and trending
- Leadership must allocate adequate resources to support medication error prevention and management
- Leadership must coordinate medication management with Quality Assessment and Performance Improvement (QAPI) program

---

## Immediate Reporting Requirements

### 1. Immediate Physician Notification

#### When Errors Must Be Reported:
- Drug preparation errors
- Drug administration errors
- Medication prescribing errors
- Adverse drug reactions
- Drug incompatibilities/interactions

#### Reporting Protocol:
- **Errors must be immediately reported to the attending physician** if appropriate
- If the attending physician is unavailable, the covering physician shall be notified
- When the covering physician is notified due to unavailability of the attending physician:
  - The patient's attending physician shall be notified as soon as they are available
- Errors, reactions, and incompatibilities must be recorded in the patient's medical record

### 2. Documentation Requirements
- Information from errors and reactions must be documented
- Documentation must be completed promptly following the error
- All errors must be recorded in the patient's medical record

---

## Tracking and Aggregation Systems

### 1. Systematic Tracking
- All medication-related errors shall be tracked and maintained
- Errors must be tracked in an organized system that allows for retrieval and analysis
- The tracking system must permit the hospital to:
  - Organize data by type of error/adverse event
  - Organize data by dates to show trends over time
  - Organize data by shift, unit, or other relevant variables

### 2. Procedures for Reporting Aggregation
- Procedures must be in place for reporting transfusion reactions, adverse drug reactions, and errors in:
  - Prescribing medications
  - Preparing medications
  - Administering medications
- These errors and reactions shall be aggregated in the aggregate for trending and analysis
- Information and related data shall be compiled and organized for meaningful analysis

### 3. Trending and Analysis
- Organization must have a means for aggregating data and related information
- Data must be trended over time to identify patterns and trends
- Data must be analyzed to identify opportunities for improvement
- Analysis must be periodically evaluated to identify and implement corrective/preventive action

---

## Investigative Processes

### 1. Systematic Cause Analysis

#### Root Cause Analysis (RCA):
- Hospitals should analyze medication errors using a systemic approach to determine root causes
- Analysis must examine:
  - System factors that contributed to the error
  - Process weaknesses
  - Equipment or device issues
  - Communication failures
  - Training or knowledge gaps
  - Environmental factors

#### Types of Medical Errors to Analyze:
Common medication errors requiring analysis include:
- **Medication administration errors** - wrong medication, wrong dosage, wrong patient, wrong route, wrong time, etc.
- **Prescribing errors** - inappropriate drug selection, incorrect dosage, drug interactions not identified
- **Preparation errors** - incorrect compounding, mislabeling, contamination
- **Adverse drug reactions** - unexpected reactions requiring investigation
- **Drug incompatibilities** - incompatible medications or combinations administered

### 2. Error Classification

#### Near Misses:
- An error detected and corrected before harmful effect occurs
- Must be tracked and analyzed like other errors
- Provides valuable learning opportunity about system weaknesses
- Focus on prevention can prevent future actual harm

#### Actual Harm Errors:
- Errors that result in patient injury or adverse outcome
- Require thorough investigation and corrective action
- May require additional reporting to external agencies based on severity

#### Preventability Assessment:
- Hospitals must distinguish between:
  - **Preventable adverse events** - injury caused by an error
  - **Non-preventable adverse events** - untoward, undesirable, unanticipated event causing death or serious injury not related to error

---

## Reporting to Physician and Leadership

### 1. Attending Physician Notification
- All medication errors must be reported immediately to the attending physician
- Physician must be informed of:
  - Nature of the error
  - Medications involved
  - Patient impact (if any)
  - Actions taken
  - Current patient status

### 2. Escalation to Leadership
- Trends in medication errors must be reported to:
  - Senior leadership team
  - Quality Management Oversight
  - Medical staff leadership
  - Nursing leadership
  - Pharmacy leadership

### 3. Reporting Frequency
- Regular reporting of medication error data to organizational leaders
- Analysis findings must be presented to leadership
- Leadership must review trends periodically

---

## Prevention and Corrective Action

### 1. Developing Preventive Strategies

After systematic analysis identifies causes, hospitals must:

#### Policy and Procedure Changes:
- Implement changes to policies and procedures to prevent future errors
- Standardize medication administration practices
- Establish protocols for high-risk medications
- Create safeguards for error-prone processes

#### Equipment and Technology:
- Repair or replace faulty equipment
- Implement technology solutions (e.g., barcode scanning, computerized order entry)
- Ensure all equipment is properly maintained and monitored
- Use infusion pumps, smart pumps, and other technology to prevent dose errors

#### Safe Medication Practices:
- Label medications clearly and correctly
- Limit use of dangerous abbreviations
- Implement alert systems for look-alike and sound-alike drug names
- Use facility-approved pre-printed order sheets
- Establish dosing limits and administration guidelines for high-alert medications
- Ensure availability of up-to-date medication information
- Ensure pharmacist availability on-call when pharmacy is not 24/7

#### Education and Training:
- Provide staff education on error prevention
- Train personnel on proper medication preparation and administration
- Ensure staff understand:
  - Indications for medications
  - Potential side effects and adverse reactions
  - Drug interactions and incompatibilities
  - Dose limits and parameters
  - Special procedures or techniques required
- Document staff training completion

#### System Improvements:
- Limit variety of medication-related devices and equipment
- Implement standardized medication ordering processes
- Establish protocols prohibiting orders to "resume previous orders"
- Create inventory management systems to prevent stockouts that lead to emergency situations
- Improve communication processes between prescribers, pharmacists, and nurses

### 2. Communication of Improvements
- Make all affected staff aware of strategies and actions implemented
- Provide applicable training on new processes
- Post updates and reminders about error prevention
- Use newsletters, staff meetings, and educational materials to communicate changes

### 3. Documentation of Changes
- Document all implemented changes
- Maintain evidence of:
  - Documented staff education and training
  - New or revised policies
  - Equipment repairs or replacements
  - Process improvements
  - Technology implementations

---

## Effectiveness Monitoring and Sustained Improvement

### 1. Measuring Effectiveness
- Hospital must have a method to assess whether implemented strategies resulted in improved outcomes
- Must continue periodic data collection on targeted areas
- Data collection must enable hospital to determine whether:
  - Specific indicators show improvement after implementation
  - Improvements are sustained over time
  - Additional interventions are needed

#### Benchmarking:
- Hospitals should compare their medication error identification rates to established benchmarks
- Use studies on reporting rates published in peer-reviewed journals
- Utilize resources from the Institute for Safe Medication Practices (ISMP)
- Consider factors such as hospital size and scope of services

### 2. Adequacy of Reporting System
- Hospital must determine whether its reporting system identifies:
  - As many medication errors and adverse drug reactions as expected
  - Errors that would be expected for the size and scope of services provided
- If perception is that medication errors are under-reported:
  - Determine actions hospital is taking to ensure accurate reporting
  - Assess staff awareness of internal reporting process

### 3. Evaluation and Adjustment
- Periodically re-evaluate medication administration timing policies
- Assess adherence to established policies
- Review medication errors related to timing and analyze causes
- Adjust policies and procedures based on evaluation results
- If improvements are not sustained:
  - Implement new or revised strategy
  - Conduct additional data collection and monitoring
  - Evaluate effectiveness of new solution

### 4. Continuous Monitoring
- Ongoing tracking of medication error data
- Monitoring of specific indicators such as:
  - Blood product transfusion reactions
  - Drug reactions
  - Errors in medication administration
  - Infection control-related errors

---

## Patient and Family Communication

### 1. Policy Development
- Organization must develop policy and procedures for informing patients and families about unexpected adverse events
- Communication process must address:
  - Who will communicate with patient/family
  - When communication will occur
  - What information will be shared
  - How communication will be documented

### 2. Communication Principles
- Communication should demonstrate that hospital is acting responsibly
- Communication does not imply wrongdoing on the part of hospital or staff
- Focus on promoting safest environment possible
- Patients have the right to know the circumstances of events
- Process identifies most effective way of responding to events

### 3. Patient Rights
- Patients must be informed of unexpected adverse events
- Family members must have opportunity to understand what occurred
- Clear explanation of:
  - What happened
  - Why it happened
  - What is being done to address it
  - What steps will prevent future occurrences

---

## Non-Punitive Reporting System

### 1. Promoting Accurate Reporting
- Hospital should adopt a **non-punitive reporting system**
- Focus must be on the **system and not the involved healthcare professionals**
- Encourage and reward identification of errors and near misses
- Promote open discussion of errors and system improvements

### 2. Staff Engagement
- A voluntary, non-punitive reporting system for monitoring and reporting:
  - Adverse drug events
  - Medication errors
  - Adverse drug reactions
- Staff should feel safe reporting errors without fear of punishment
- Focus must be on learning and improving systems, not on individual blame

### 3. Culture Change
- Organizational leadership must support non-punitive approach
- Communicate that errors are opportunities for system improvement
- Recognize that most errors are due to system failures, not individual carelessness
- Encourage staff to participate in error investigation and improvement

---

## Quality Management Integration

### 1. QAPI Program Integration
- Medication errors must be integrated into QAPI program
- All medication-related issues must be:
  - Communicated to QAPI leadership
  - Addressed in collaboration with QAPI program
  - Included in quality improvement initiatives

### 2. Leadership Coordination
- Governing body, medical staff, and director of nursing must ensure:
  - QAPI program addresses problems identified through medication error analysis
  - In-service training programs address errors identified
  - Leadership is jointly responsible for linking medication error data with QAPI program
  - Corrective action plans are implemented and monitored

### 3. Training Program Integration
- In-service training programs must address:
  - Medication errors identified in organization
  - Corrective actions implemented
  - Prevention strategies
  - Lessons learned from errors

---

## Key Medication Error Prevention Practices

### 1. Safe Ordering Practices
- Use facility-approved pre-printed order sheets whenever possible
- Prohibit orders to "resume previous orders"
- Ensure prescriber orders include:
  - Patient identification
  - Medication name
  - Strength and dosage
  - Route of administration
  - Frequency and timing
  - Indication for use (when appropriate)
  - Prescriber signature or electronic authentication

### 2. Safe Preparation Practices
- All compounding, packaging, and dispensing of medication under pharmacist supervision
- Staff involved in compounding must be trained and competent
- Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) must be followed
- All materials, equipment, and environment must meet standards
- Personnel must use appropriate personal protective equipment

### 3. Safe Administration Practices
- **Five Rights of Medication Administration must be verified:**
  1. **Right Patient** - Confirm patient identity using acceptable identifiers (full name, ID number, date of birth)
  2. **Right Medication** - Ensure medication matches prescription
  3. **Right Dose** - Verify dosage matches prescription and is appropriate/safe
  4. **Right Route** - Ensure method of administration is appropriate
  5. **Right Time** - Administer at appropriate time per policy

#### Additional Safe Administration Practices:
- Verify patient identity on wrist band, ID card, or patient statement before administration
- Match medication to administration record and labeling
- Remain with patient until medication is taken
- Document administration in patient medical record
- Address missed or late doses according to policy

### 4. Timing Management
- **Time-Critical Medications** (early or late administration >30 minutes could cause harm):
  - Administer within 30 minutes before or after scheduled time (1-hour window total)
  - Examples: antibiotics, anticoagulants, insulin, anticonvulsants, pain medications
- **Non-Time-Critical Medications**:
  - Daily or longer administration: within 2 hours before or after (4-hour window total)
  - More frequent than daily but no more than every 4 hours: within 1 hour before or after (2-hour window total)
- **Not Eligible for Scheduled Times**:
  - Stat doses, first doses, one-time doses, as-needed (PRN) doses
  - Ensure timely administration per prescriber intent

### 5. High-Alert Medication Management
- Identify high-alert medications that carry increased risk of harm
- Establish specific:
  - Dosing limits
  - Administration guidelines
  - Packaging standards
  - Labeling requirements
  - Storage protocols
- Ensure staff are trained on high-alert medications
- Implement additional safeguards and verification steps

### 6. Drug Interaction and Compatibility Monitoring
- Ensure up-to-date drug information is available to all staff
- Screen for drug interactions and incompatibilities
- Verify compatibility of medications prior to administration
- Report incompatibilities immediately to prescriber
- Implement computerized checking systems when possible

---

## Medication Monitoring After Administration

### 1. Clinical Monitoring
- Monitor effects of medication on patient to ensure therapy is appropriate
- Assess clinical data to evaluate medication efficacy
- Monitor for signs of toxicity and adverse effects
- Assess patient's own perceptions about side effects and efficacy
- Collect and review relevant laboratory data

### 2. Adverse Reaction Identification
- Train staff to identify adverse drug reactions
- Document all adverse reactions
- Report reactions immediately to attending physician
- Implement corrective actions as needed
- Track all reactions for trending and analysis

### 3. Patient Education
- Educate patients on medications prescribed
- Inform patients of expected effects and side effects
- Train patients on proper administration techniques
- Teach patients to report adverse reactions or concerns
- Provide written materials for patient reference

## Specific Requirements for Medication Errors

### 1. Immediate Actions Upon Error Discovery
- Stop administration if error has not yet occurred
- If patient has already received erroneous medication:
  - Notify attending physician immediately
  - Document all details of error
  - Monitor patient closely for adverse effects
  - Implement appropriate medical interventions as needed
  - Record all actions taken in medical record

### 2. Reporting Documentation
- Complete incident report with details including:
  - Patient involved
  - What error occurred (medication name, dosage, route, etc.)
  - When error occurred and was discovered
  - How error was discovered
  - Who discovered the error
  - What actions were taken
  - Patient response/status
  - Notification to physician

### 3. Near Miss Documentation
- Document near misses even if no patient harm occurred
- Analyze near misses to identify system vulnerabilities
- Implement preventive measures based on near miss analysis
- Use near misses as learning opportunities

- Participate in error investigation and analysis

## Summary of Key Principles

1. **Immediate Reporting** - Medication errors must be reported immediately to the attending physician
2. **Systematic Tracking** - All errors must be tracked in an organized system allowing for analysis
3. **Root Cause Analysis** - Errors must be investigated using systematic approaches to identify causes
4. **Preventive Action** - Comprehensive strategies must be implemented to prevent future errors
5. **Non-Punitive Culture** - Reporting systems must be non-punitive with focus on system improvement
6. **Staff Education** - All staff must receive training on medication error prevention and safe practices
7. **Integration with QAPI** - Medication errors must be integrated into quality improvement initiatives
8. **Patient Monitoring** - Close monitoring of patients who experience errors to detect adverse effects
9. **Family Communication** - Patients and families must be informed of unexpected adverse events
10. **Sustained Improvement** - Ongoing monitoring and evaluation to ensure improvements are sustained
11. **Leadership Accountability** - Governing body and medical staff must ensure program effectiveness
12. **Five Rights Verification** - Right patient, medication, dose, route, and time must be verified for every administration

# 8. Explain the patient rights and responsibilities outlined in the standards?

## Answer

According to NIAHO standards, patient rights and responsibilities are outlined primarily in the Patient Rights (PR) chapter. Here are the key requirements:

### Informed Decision-Making
Patients have the right to participate and make informed decisions regarding their plan of care (PR.2 SR.2). Patients or their representatives have the right to be informed of their health status, be involved in care planning and treatment, and request or refuse treatment (PR.2 SR.3). Patients have the right to have a family member or representative of their choice and to have their own physician notified promptly of their admission (PR.2 SR.4).

### Visitation Rights
The organization must inform each patient of their visitation rights, including any clinical restrictions or limitations (PR.2 SR.12b). Patients have the right to receive visitors whom they designate, including spouses, domestic partners (including same-sex partners), family members, or friends, and may withdraw or deny such consent at any time (PR.2 SR.12c). All visitors must enjoy full and equal visitation privileges consistent with patient preferences (PR.2 SR.12e).

### Notification Requirements
The organization must inform patients and legal representatives of their rights in advance of providing or discontinuing care, using standardized notice forms (PR.2 SR.13).

### Additional Rights
Other rights are defined within the broader Patient Rights requirements (PR.1 through PR.10), which cover additional protections and entitlements for patients receiving care.

The standards require that hospitals communicate patient rights to both inpatients and outpatients and ensure these rights are accessible to all patients, including those with communication, hearing, or cognitive limitations.

## Answer

### LS.2: POTENTIALLY INFECTIOUS BLOOD AND PRODUCTS

**Definition:** Potentially human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) infectious blood and blood components can come from a donor who: tested negative at the time of donation but tests reactive for evidence of HIV infection on a later donation; tests positive on the supplemental (additional, more specific) test or other follow-up testing required by FDA; and for whom the timing of seroconversion cannot be precisely estimated. Potentially hepatitis C virus (HCV) infectious blood and blood components are the blood and blood components identified in 21 CFR 610.47.

#### Standards Requirements

- SR.1 If an organization regularly uses the services of an outside blood bank, it shall have an agreement with the blood bank that governs the procurement, transfer, and availability of blood and blood products.
- SR.2 The agreement shall require that the blood bank promptly notify the organization of the following:
- SR.2a Within three calendar days if the blood bank supplied blood and blood products collected from a donor who tested negative at the time of donation but tests repeatedly reactive for the antibody to HIV or HCV on a later donation; and,
- SR.2b The results of the FDA licensed more specific test or other follow-up testing recommended or required by the FDA completed within 45 calendar days after the donor's repeatedly reactive screening test for HIV or HCV.
- SR.2c Within 3 calendar days after the blood bank supplied blood and blood components collected from an infectious donor, whenever such records are available.
- SR.2d Quarantine of blood and blood products pending completion of testing: If the blood bank notifies the organization of the repeatedly reactive HIV or HCV screening test results, the organization shall determine the disposition of the blood or blood product and quarantine all blood and blood products from previous donations in inventory.
- SR.3 If the blood bank notifies the organization that the result of the FDA-licensed, more specific test or other follow up testing recommended or required by FDA is negative, absent other informative test results, the organization may release the blood and blood products from quarantine.
- SR.4 If the blood bank notifies the organization that the result of the FDA-licensed, more specific test or other follow up testing recommended or required by FDA is positive, the organization shall dispose of the blood and blood products and notify the transfusion recipients according to LS.3.
- SR.5 If the blood bank notifies the organization that the result of the FDA-licensed, more specific test or other follow up testing recommended or required by FDA is indeterminate, the organization shall destroy or label prior collections of blood and blood products held in quarantine (as set forth at 21 CFR 610.46(b)(2) and 610.47(b)(2)).
- SR.6 The organization shall maintain adequate records which identify the source and disposition of all units of blood and blood components for no less than 10 years from the date of disposition in manner reflecting QM.2 (SR.3b) and are stored in such a manner they are available for prompt retrieval.
- SR.6a The organization will have a plan in place to transfer these records to another hospital or other entity if the hospital ceases its operations for any reason. The organization will have allocated adequate funding to execute this plan when necessary.

- SR.1d Corrective and preventive actions are implemented, measured and monitored for effectiveness and sustainability.
- SR.1e As a part of the QMS for addressing performance improvement and patient safety, the organization shall select projects or similar activities that focus attention on various processes, functions and areas of the organization.
- SR.1e(1) The number and scope of these projects or similar activities will be conducted annually and be proportional to the scope and complexity of the organization's operations and services offered.
- SR.1e(1)(i) A hospital may, as one of its projects, develop and implement an information technology system explicitly designed to improve patient safety and quality of care. This project, in its initial stage of development, does not need to demonstrate measurable improvement in indicators related to health outcomes.
- SR.1e(1)(ii) For hospitals that offer obstetrical services, the hospital shall conduct at least one measurable performance improvement project focused on improving health outcomes and disparities among the hospital's population(s) of obstetrical patients annually. (Note: This requirement shall be implemented by January 1, 2027)
- SR.1e(2) These projects or similar activities will be documented to include the rationale for selection and measurable progress achieved.
- SR.1e(3) If the organization participates in a Quality Improvement Organization (QIO) cooperative project, the organization shall demonstrate that information and supporting documentation is provided to the QIO. If the organization does not participate in a QIO, the projects and activities are required to be of comparable effort.
- SR.2 In addition to any other Quality Management System standard, the organization is required to comply with QM.1 at all times as a part of its Quality Management System. Until the organization achieves ISO 9001 Compliance/Certification, the organization shall follow at a minimum the ISO 9001 methodology specified in QM.2 (SR.3).

#### Interpretive Guidelines

The organization's leadership, meaning the hospital's governing body (or organized group or individual who assumes full legal authority and responsibility for operations of the hospital), medical staff, and senior leadership, are responsible and accountable for the hospital's QMS. The medical staff may delegate this leadership responsibility and accountability for the QAPI program to the medical staff executive committee if it has such a committee. Senior leadership includes, at a minimum, the hospital's chief executive officer, chief operating officer, and the chief nurse executive (or equivalent), but would also include other executives in the hospital's administration. Responsibilities include (see also QM.6, QM.7, QM.8):

- Periodic review of the QMS
- Development of a plan to implement and maintain the QMS
- Review of the progress of QAPI projects
- Determination of annual QAPI projects
- Evaluation of the effectiveness of improvement actions that the hospital has implemented
- Ensuring that clear expectations for safety are established and communicated hospital-wide, as well as allocating adequate resources to carry out the functions of the QMS and QAPI program requirements.

Quality Management System (QMS) is defined by ISO 9000 as part of a management system with regard to quality. A management system is a set of interrelated or interacting elements of an organization to establish policies, objectives, and processes to achieve those objectives. The management system elements establish the organization's structure, roles and responsibilities, planning, operation, policies, practices, rules, beliefs, objectives and processes to achieve those objectives.

The quality assessment and performance improvement (QAPI) program shall be integrated into the organization's QMS. When hospital leadership evaluate the QMS, this inherently includes the QAPI program. The terms "QMS" and "QAPI" are used interchangeably throughout the QM chapter. Where "QMS" is listed, this also includes the QAPI program.

The QAPI program shall provide a mechanism to systematically examine the quality of care delivered and implement specific improvement projects on an ongoing basis for all of the services provided by the hospital. The organization develops its program based on its unique needs, priorities, clinical programs, as well as its own considerations for the health equity needs of its patient population. There shall be processes in place to continually identify opportunities for quality and safety improvements and to implement changes that lead to improved outcomes that are sustained over time.

The resources dedicated to the QMS (and QAPI program) should be commensurate with the overall scope and complexity of the services provided by the hospital. The hospital's governing body, in collaboration with senior leadership and medical staff, should allocate adequate resources to carry out the functions specified in the QM chapter. Adequate resources would mean that the hospital provides sufficient numbers of staff (including consultants, as needed), staff time, information systems, and education and training to support all elements of the required quality and performance improvement activities and projects. In addition, adequate means that staff should be qualified to perform the functions to which they are assigned. Qualified also means staff have experience or training in the functions for which they are responsible. The hospital may choose to use qualified and experienced contractors for the day-to-day technical aspects of the program, such as data collection and analysis. However, the hospital's governing body retains the responsibility for the ongoing management of the program, even when a contractor is used for those functions. CMS does not prescribe a particular formula for determining whether the hospital has allocated adequate resources to its program. However, hospitals should be able to demonstrate inclusion of the program in its budget process and identify in detail the resources it dedicates to the program. This includes the resources that a contracted service has allocated to support these functions. Additionally, the hospital must be able to provide evidence of the number of staff it has allocated to focus on the management and oversight of the day-to-day program functions.

Performance improvement projects are differentiated from performance improvement activities (such as that under QM.7) in that performance improvement projects require a significant amount of up-front planning, include project objectives, and have a definitive beginning and end date (time-limited). Performance improvement activities make up the continuous, ongoing functions of a hospital QMS related to quality and patient safety, such as ongoing tracking of medical errors and adverse events, analysis of data, implementation of changes with associated education and training, continuous monitoring of quality and safety in all hospital departments and service areas, etc.

CMS does not prescribe the specific types of performance improvement projects to be conducted annually. The hospital's governing body determines the number and types of annual projects based on the complexity and scope of the services provided by the hospital. No fixed ratio is required, but it is acceptable for smaller hospitals with a smaller number of distinct services to have fewer projects than a large hospital with many different services.

The hospital may choose to participate in Quality Improvement Organization (QIO) projects to fulfill the annual project requirement but are not required to do so to be compliant with the requirement at QM.1 (SR.1a). QIOs are funded by CMS to promote, through cooperative projects, improvements in services provided by Medicare-participating providers. If the hospital does not participate in a QIO project, it is expected to implement its own annual projects that are comparable in effort to a QIO project. The hospital should consider the number of patients affected, the range of services covered, and the projected magnitude of the benefit to individual patients when developing annual projects. (68 FR at 3441)

The hospital shall keep records on each performance improvement project completed within the previous survey cycle, as well as a list of projects currently underway. The documentation for each project shall, at a minimum, include an explanation of why the project was undertaken. The explanation of the project shall indicate what data was collected in the hospital, or what publicly available data and/or recommendations of nationally recognized organizations, leads the hospital to believe that the project activities will result in improvements in patient health outcomes and safety in the hospital. For projects that are in progress, the hospital shall be able to explain what activities the project entails and how the impact of the project is being monitored. The hospital will provide evidence of baseline data it is collecting (or will be collecting, in the case of projects just beginning) that will enable the hospital to assess whether the project achieved measurable outcomes. For projects that are completed, the hospital should be able to demonstrate that the project resulted in measurable progress toward improving the quality of care or patient safety.

#### Surveyor Guidance

Interview leadership and review program documents (Quality Management Oversight meeting minutes, Medical Staff or MEC meeting minutes, governing body meeting minutes, etc.) to evaluate how priorities are established, how data is collected and used to monitor and improve performance, patient safety, and overall quality of patient care and operations, and to establish executive leadership's oversight of the QMS.

While these leaders are not expected to be directly involved in the day-to-day activities of the hospital's QAPI program, they should be actively engaged in the oversight of the QAPI program through their periodic review of the program, including, but not limited to:

- Development of a plan (e.g., Quality Plan, QAPI plan) to implement and maintain the QMS
- Review of the progress of QAPI projects
- Determination of annual QAPI projects
- Evaluation of the effectiveness of improvement actions that the hospital has implemented
- Planning for QAPI resources

Ask to see evidence that executive leadership makes the required decisions related to planning, data collection, and projects, conducts regular reviews of information on the performance of the QMS, and makes decisions based on that review for the overall direction and management of the QMS (see also QM.2 (SR.3f), QM.6 and QM.7).

Ask the hospital to provide a list of distinct performance improvement projects the hospital is currently conducting and has conducted since the time of the last survey to verify the hospital is conducting annual QAPI projects.

Review documentation to validate why each project was conducted and as evidence to support the progress being made on each project.

- Does the documentation include data to support "why" each project was conducted (e.g., medical error and adverse event reports indicated a need for improvement in a particular area)?
- Does the documentation include evidence of ongoing monitoring of the project's progress, such as periodic data collection and analysis?

Ask the executive leadership to explain how the selection (number and scope) of the specific projects is in alignment with the hospital's complexity and the scope of services it provides.

Consider the size of the facility and the intensity of its services, such as critical care services/units, complex surgeries, transplant services, maternal/child health services, and oncology services, including radiation and chemotherapy, etc.

## QM.5 CORRECTIVE ACTION

### Standard Requirements

**SR.1** Variations, deficiencies or non-conformities identified by the organization shall be addressed. Appropriate corrective or preventive action shall be determined, applied, and documented. Documentation of activities may take the form of a Failure Mode and Effects Analysis, Root Cause Analysis, Performance Report, Non-conformity Report, specific Performance Improvement Project analysis, etc.

**SR.2** Documentation of corrective action considered by the organization as high-risk shall become part of the Quality Management Oversight.

**SR.3** All implemented corrective action plans, regardless of risk, shall be validated for effectiveness.

### Interpretive Guidelines

The organization is to have identified, applied and documented nonconformity (non-compliance) throughout the organization and the subsequent corrective/preventive action(s) taken. The organization can demonstrate this in various ways, but there shall be information present that validates that the organization has corrected the nonconformity and that the action(s) implemented have been effective and sustained. The organization shall be able to demonstrate that planned actions were effective by quantifiable measurement subject to internal reviews (internal audits) or other means.

The organization shall have a process for elevation of high-risk corrective action that has been verified to be ineffective.

### Surveyor Guidance

Review examples of the following: Nonconformity Report, Root Cause Analysis, Failure Mode and Effects Analysis, or other documents that the organization can demonstrate a means of recording non-conformity and the subsequent corrective action and follow-up to determine that the action(s) taken have been effective. If there are different means for reporting nonconformity and/or corrective action, the surveyor will determine the consistency of the process to ensure its effectiveness.

## QM.8 PATIENT SAFETY SYSTEM

### Standard Requirements

**SR.1** The organization shall have a means for establishing clear expectations for identifying and detecting the prevalence and severity of incidents that impact or threaten patient safety. This shall include medical errors and adverse patient events.

**SR.2** The organization's patient safety system shall be documented and shall address the following:

**SR.2a** Detection, reporting, investigation and response to medical errors and adverse patient events;

**SR.2b** Aggregation, trending, and analysis of data;

**SR.2c** Implementation of preventative and corrective action;

**SR.2d** Defined processes to reduce risk;

**SR.2e** On-going measurement to ensure action effectiveness;

**SR.2f** Feedback and learning throughout the hospital;

**SR.2g** Policy and procedure of informing patients and/or their families about unexpected adverse events.

**SR.3** Trends in medical errors, adverse patient events, or other process errors/problems reported in the organization's incident reporting system shall be reported to the senior leadership team (through management review and/or Quality Management Oversight).

### Interpretive Guidelines

In certain circumstances, there are incidents that impact or threaten patient safety. It is the responsibility of the organization to develop means of controlling processes to ensure the processes are safe for patients and staff as they are carried out.

The reporting mechanism for medical errors and adverse events is at the discretion of the hospital. The system shall permit the hospital to track and analyze medical errors and adverse events in an effective and meaningful manner. As appropriate, the hospital should educate all hospital staff and contract staff on what is considered a medical error and an adverse event, as well as when and how to report these events. All staff (including contractors) should be able to articulate their roles and responsibilities in supporting the hospital's expectations for safety, such as what safety risks or breaches they are expected to report and how they would be expected to report them. Hospitals may communicate safety expectations through education and training, the use of posters that are constant reminders of safety requirements, staff newsletters, etc.

#### Definition of Medical Error

For purposes of this section, error means the failure of a planned action to be completed as intended or the use of a wrong plan to achieve an aim. Errors can include problems in practice, products, procedures, and systems. Medical error means an error that occurs in the delivery of healthcare services.

#### Types of Medical Errors

There are many types of medical errors, including, but not limited to:

- **Medication administration errors** - wrong medication, wrong dosage, wrong patient, etc.
- **Surgical errors** - wrong patient, wrong site, retained instrument, etc.
- **Equipment failure** – defibrillator without working batteries, IV pump that results in inadvertent dosing, alarms not working properly, etc.
- **Infection control errors** – poor aseptic technique, incorrect processing of sterile instruments and equipment, incorrect isolation practices, lack of standard precautions, etc.
- **Blood transfusion-related errors** – wrong patient, wrong blood product administered, etc.
- **Diagnostic errors** – misdiagnoses leading to incorrect choice of therapy, failure to use an indicated diagnostic test, misinterpretation of test results, failure to act properly on abnormal results.

#### Near Misses and Adverse Events

Not every medical error results in harm to a patient; the error may be detected and addressed before a harmful effect can occur (a "near miss") or the consequence of the error may be minimal. From a patient safety perspective, a near miss is considered an error and much can be learned from the near miss in terms of system weaknesses that could, in the future, result in actual harm to patients. Therefore, hospitals must track and analyze errors that result in near misses and focus on their prevention and reduction. While the regulation also specifically calls for measuring, analyzing, and tracking of adverse events, adverse events may or may not be preventable. A preventable adverse event is an injury caused by an error. Section 482.70 defines an adverse event as "an untoward, undesirable, and usually unanticipated event that causes death or serious injury, or the risk thereof."

#### Analysis of Causes of Medical Errors and Adverse Events

Hospitals should analyze medical errors and adverse events to determine the cause(s) by using a systemic approach for determining the cause of the error and for implementing appropriate preventive actions.

#### Implementing Improvement/Preventive Strategies

After the systemic analysis of the error or adverse event has led to the identification of the cause of the event, hospitals should develop and implement preventive actions to improve the quality and safety associated with the event or the error. Preventive actions include, but are not limited to, changes in policies and procedures, repairing or replacing equipment, staff education and training, etc. Where appropriate, the hospital should make all affected staff aware of the strategies and related actions it has implemented to correct and prevent specific errors and adverse events and also provide applicable training. Hospitals should be able to provide evidence of the implemented changes, such as documented staff education and training, documentation of new or revised policies, evidence that equipment has been repaired or replaced, etc. The organization should identify, implement and regularly assess the means by which these incidents are prevented or when they occur. The incidents are studied to detect nonconformance and where risk points or failures are an inherent part of the process and work to remove these risk points or failures from the system.

Where process variation and deficiencies are detected, appropriate corrective action shall be determined, applied, and documented (QM.5 (SR.1)). Implemented corrective action shall be validated for effectiveness (QM.5 (SR.3)).

#### Evaluating Changes and Sustaining Improvement

The hospital should also have a method to assess whether the strategies and actions it has implemented resulted in improved outcomes and that those improved outcomes are sustained over time. This means the hospital should collect data that enables the hospital to determine whether indicators, related to a specific area targeted for change, actually demonstrated an improvement after implementation of the changes. For example, the hospital should continue to periodically collect data on proper hand hygiene and then analyze the data to determine if the solution has resulted in sustained improvement in handwashing compliance. If the analysis of the periodic collection of handwashing data shows that the solution has not resulted in a sustained improvement over an appropriate period of time, a new or revised strategy/solution must be implemented, with subsequent data collection and monitoring to evaluate the effectiveness of the new solution.

#### Prospective Hospitals Applying for Initial Certification in Medicare

A facility seeking Medicare program initial certification as a hospital may not have been in operation long enough to demonstrate extensive internal data collection for the identification of opportunities for improvement based on the monitoring data. However, it must be able to show that it has an active data collection and analysis infrastructure in place and indicate when it expects to have sufficient data to begin analysis. In addition, because hospitals may utilize quality indicators from outside sources to prioritize QAPI program activities, an initial applicant would still be expected to provide evidence of implementing improvement actions based on selected indicators from outside sources.

#### Patient and Family Communication

When such incidents occur, a process shall be in place to address customer (patient) communication, how the patients are informed and their right to know the circumstances of events. Such communication does not imply wrongdoing on the part of the hospital or its staff members. The process identifies the most effective way of responding to such events. The process also requires a level of communication for the customer (patient) to know that the hospital is acting responsibly and will promote the safest environment possible.

### Surveyor Guidance

The organization's creation of an environment that is safe for patients and staff is imperative. Assess the ability of the organization to detect and prevent adverse patient events, act accordingly to improve these processes through corrective/preventive action and monitoring the effectiveness of their efforts. This could be done by reviewing root cause analyses and/or failure mode and effects analysis where such processes or events have been studied and the associated documentation to support findings, corrective/preventive action(s) taken and the follow-up to determine their effectiveness.

Ask to see evidence that the hospital tracks data for the identified indicators, which may include, but are not limited to blood product transfusion reactions, drug reactions, errors in medication administration, and infection control-related errors and events.

Ask to see evidence of the incident reporting system and related policies for reporting and responding to incidents/events. Ask for a demonstration of how to use the system and how the system is able to organize the reported data for meaningful analysis.

#### System Data Organization Questions

- Can the system organize the data by type of error/adverse event?
- Can the system organize the data by dates to show trends over time?
- Can the system organize the data by shift, by unit where the error occurred, etc.?

#### Evidence of Corrective Actions

Select a sample of several (at least three) adverse events or errors the hospital has tracked and ask to see written evidence it has used a systemic approach (e.g., root cause analysis (RCA)) to analyze the cause of the events and errors), implemented changes based on the identified causes to prevent further events or errors, conducted periodic data collection to verify if the changes resulted in improvements, and analyzed the post-implementation data to assess whether the improvement (if there was an improvement) was sustained over time.

Ask the hospital leadership who oversee the patient safety system to provide evidence of improvement activities that have been initiated based on data reported through the incident reporting system.

Ask to see evidence of hospital-wide staff education and training regarding what errors and adverse events must be reported and how to report them. Look at the materials used for education and training. Are there training records to show staff received the training?

Interview staff in various units to assess their understanding of identifying and reporting medical errors and adverse events.

Review the organization's process for reporting medical errors and adverse patient events (e.g., incident reporting system). Review the process for compliance to QM.8 (SR.2a-2g and SR.3).

Ensure the corrective action process for detecting process variation and deficiencies aligns with QM.5.

# 12. Chapters related to patient safety
## SUMMARY OF PATIENT SAFETY STRUCTURE

### Key Patient Safety Frameworks

The healthcare standards organize patient safety through four interconnected chapters:

1. **QM.4 - Management Representative**: Designates leadership responsibility for QMS implementation and patient safety oversight

2. **QM.5 - Corrective Action**: Establishes processes for identifying, analyzing, and correcting safety problems using Root Cause Analysis, Failure Mode & Effects Analysis, and other systematic approaches

3. **QM.8 - Patient Safety System**: Creates the core patient safety system with:
   - Detection and reporting of errors and adverse events
   - Data aggregation and trending
   - Preventive and corrective actions
   - Risk reduction processes
   - Effectiveness measurement
   - Organization-wide feedback and learning
   - Patient/family communication about adverse events

### Critical Patient Safety Elements

**Identification and Reporting**
- Clear expectations for detecting patient safety incidents
- Mandatory reporting mechanisms for medical errors and adverse events
- Staff education on what to report and how

**Analysis and Action**
- Systematic root cause analysis
- Identification of system weaknesses
- Implementation of preventive actions
- Documentation of changes and staff training

**Measurement and Sustainability**
- Ongoing data collection
- Trend analysis over time
- Validation of corrective action effectiveness
- Sustained improvement verification

**Communication**
- Hospital-wide feedback and learning
- Staff training and awareness
- Patient and family notification of unexpected adverse events
- Leadership reporting to senior management and Quality Management Oversight
